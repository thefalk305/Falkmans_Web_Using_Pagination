A city with the representation of literally hundreds of ethnic groups, Chicago has rightfully earned its nickname as "The Melting Pot of America". This is where, in the early 1890's, Nils Falkman and his family finally settled. Here is were they set down their roots. Many generations of Falkman's were born here and still live in and around the Chicagoland area.

Lake Michigan is Chicago's most iconic geographical feature, providing 26 miles of stunning shoreline and numerous public beaches, connected by the scenic Lakefront Trail. It is a popular spot for swimming, sunbathing, cycling, and dining. The lake is also essential to Chicago's identity and economy, offering recreational activities and a connection to a vast waterway that extends through out the Great Lakes and even to the Atlantic Ocean.

In the mid-1900s, Chicago’s baseball scene revolved around two iconic ballparks that embodied the city’s love of the game: Wrigley Field on the North Side and Comiskey Park on the South Side. Wrigley, home of the Cubs, was known for its ivy-covered outfield walls, intimate neighborhood setting, and the loyal fans who packed its stands despite the team’s long struggles. Across town, Comiskey Park, where the White Sox played, stood as a massive concrete-and-steel structure that hosted some of the era’s great games and was famous for its exploding scoreboard and lively South Side crowds. Both parks were deeply tied to their surrounding communities, serving not only as sporting venues but also as gathering places where Chicagoans from all walks of life came together, creating a citywide baseball rivalry that helped define Chicago’s mid-century identity.
